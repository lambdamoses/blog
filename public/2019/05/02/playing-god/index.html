Playing God?

May 2, 2019 in Science and Religion, Autism

This is nothing new. Procedures like abortion, germ line gene editing, in vitro fertilization (IVF), pre-implantation genetic diagnosis (PGD), and human embryonic stem cell (hESC) research cause quite a bit of discomfort, especially among religious people. On the one hand, the discomfort comes from the destruction of embryos, which are considered human beings, involved in those procedures. On the other hand, many people would say scientists are playing God by meddling with creation of human beings. Related to the allegation of “playing God” is the prospect of designer babies and thus a new era of eugenics.

An in depth study conducted by the research group of Elain Howard Ecklund about how religious Americans think of science confirms those two reasons for discomfort (besides, this study debunks some stereotypes about religious people, such as that religious people are uneducated about science). In short, two issues frame various religious perspectives about science and technology: sovereignty of God and sacredness of human life.

What this study also found is that those who are theologically conservative lean towards a “creator theology”, namely that “God’s role as master creator and maintains firmly established boundaries and a clear hierarchy between God’s role and the human role”1. In this view, technologies like PGD, IVF, and hESC research would cross this line and place humans in a God-like role, and would thus usurp God’s role as creator. In contrast, liberals prefer to view humans as co-creators with God and technologies like IVF, PGD, and hESC research as means God gave us to alleviate human suffering2. This study also found differing attitudes towards suffering. While most religious people want to alleviate suffering, some view suffering as part of the human experience3. Some would accept children with disease as part of God’s plan rather than use technology to prevent such children from being born1.

What do I think of “playing God”?

In short, I don’t like the popular usage of this term. However, though I identify as liberal, I do think we should learn from the concerns of conservatives when it comes to playing God; those who are theologically liberal do have legitimate reasons to worry about playing God. I will come to that later.

First of all, I do not find human’s ability to interfere with creation of human beings really playing God. Put more bluntly, even if we can create new universes, we still can’t really play God. From classical theology, here is the ontological difference between God and us: God is the unconditional reality, while everything else is contingent reality. Which means existence is in God’s essence, but not in our essence, so God grounds His own existence, while our existence must be grounded by something else, which would go all the way back to God. This also means that while God can create ex nihilo, we can only create something from something else. By the way, if it is true that the universe pops out of a “quantum vacuum”, this does not mean that the universe arose from nothing, because quantum vacuum is not nothing – we still need to account for the quantum vacuum itself and the natural laws governing it.

Whatever we can do, it can only be within the confines of natural laws created by God with materials created by God while God is sustaining the existence of ourselves, and gene editing and what have you do not violate natural laws or create something out of nothing. So there’s no way we can really usurp God’s role as creator. Furthermore, some theologians like Avicenna can come to terms with a universe without temporal beginning, because the existence of the eternal universe is still contingent and needs to be constantly sustained by God. In this sense, God’s creation is in every moment.

I know, as the theologian John Hick said, everything in theology is controversial. Some theologians have challenged classical theology and proposed alternative models like panentiesm and process theology, that challenge the notion of God’s immutability. Fine, we puny human minds can’t grasp God’s essence, as we Muslims believe that there’s nothing like God (Quran 6:100, 36:78, 42:11). We also find support in the Quran that however powerful we think we or our idols are, we can be no match to God, and that if God wills something, there is no possibility of countering. For instance, see

For, no single thing exists that does not have its source with Us; and nought do We bestow from on high unless it be in accordance with a measure well-defined. (15:21, Muhammad Asad)

O MEN! A parable is set forth [herewith]; hearken, then, to it! Behold, those beings whom you invoke instead of God cannot create [as much as] a fly, even were they to join all their forces to that end! And if a fly robs them of anything, they cannot [even] rescue it from him! Weak indeed is the seeker, and [weak] the sought! (22:73)

And thus it is [with most people]: thou ask them, “Who is it that has created the heavens and the earth?” - they Will surely answer, “God.” Say: “Have you, then, ever considered what it is that you invoke instead of God? If God wills that harm should befall me, could those [imaginary powers] remove the harm inflicted by Him? Or, if He wills that grace should alight on me, could they withhold His grace [from me]?” Say: “God is enough for me! In Him [alone] place their trust all who have trust [in His existence].” (39:38)

Now as for [the tribe of] ‘Ad, they walked arrogantly on earth, [offending] against all right, and saying, “Who could have a power greater than ours?” Why - were they, then, not aware that God, who created them, had a power greater than theirs? But they went on rejecting Our messages; and thereupon We let loose upon them a stormwind raging through days of misfortune, so as to give them, in the life of this world, a foretaste of suffering through humiliation: but [their] suffering in the life to come will be far more humiliating, and they will have none to succour them. (41:15-16)

Second, I find more support for the co-creator theology in the Quran than for the creator theology. In the Quran, God is said to work through humans and natural means. For instance,

And among His signs are the ships that sail like [floating] mountains through the seas: if He so wills, He stills the wind, and then they lie motionless on the sea’s surface [and] herein, behold, there are messages indeed for all who are wholly patient in adversity and deeply grateful [to God] (42:32-33)

It is God who sends forth the winds [of hope], so that they raise a cloud - whereupon He spreads it over the skies as He wills, and causes it to break up so that thou seest rain issue from within it: and as soon as He causes it to fall upon whomever He wills of His servants - lo! they rejoice (30:49)

Here, we see that God uses wind to drive ships, and even ancients know that ships are built by humans; nevertheless, ships are said to be signs of God. Note that that something has naturalistic explanations does not mean that it cannot also be a sign of God. Also, we see that God uses wind to raise clouds and eventually causing rain. This is a clear example of God working through natural forces. We also have examples of God working through humans4:

And yet, [O believers,] it was not you who slew the enemy, but it was God who slew them; and it was not thou who cast [terror into them, O Prophet], when thou didst cast it, but it was God who cast it: and [He did all this] in order that He might test the believers by a goodly test of His Own or daining. Verily, God is all-hearing, all-knowing! (8:17)

Thus, when they are told, “Spend on others out of what God has provided for you as sustenance,” those who are bent on denying the truth say unto those who believe, “Shall we feed anyone whom, if [your] God had so willed, He could have fed [Himself]? Clearly, you are but lost in error!” (36:47)

Are there verses about God being jealous about human achievements in the Quran? None. So don’t worry about technological developments as usurping God’s role as creator, as God is also creating through us and through our feats, aren’t we a better sign of God’s creativity? However if we want to be righteous servants of God, then our use of technology must be ethical. This brings us to lessons we should learn from concerns over playing God.

What to learn from concerns over playing God

Thankfulness to God

If advancement of technology itself cannot usurp God’s sovereignty, we actually do have a very real concern about playing God – the arrogant attitude that we are so great that we don’t need God, because we are effectively God. Perhaps I missed the point of conservatives without considering such attitude. It doesn’t even take much technology to do so, as a reason why ancients mentioned in the Quran reject God’s messengers is that they considered themselves God-like. Verses 41:15-16 above is one example, and Pharaoh, who claimed himself a god, is another example (28:38). Other examples are Qarun (28:78), who considered his wealth only his achievement while refusing to give thanks to God, and the wealthy gardener in Surah 18 (18:34-42), who boasted the abundance of his garden as if it will never end, while forgetting that his wealth was a blessing from God. In all these examples, in spite of the complacency, God brought the arrogant to a bleak end.

From these examples, we see how being humble and not declaring ourselves gods is closely related to how we should give thanks to God for all blessings in our lives. Also note that a theme in verses about nature is to highlight how the most backgrounded and taken for granted facts about life are in fact God’s blessings to be grateful for, from our lives itself to day and night, water, food, sun, moon, and stars, and yes, ships, and human senses and reason (e.g. 23:12-22, 15:21-23, 14:32-34). These verses about nature are interlaced with reminders of gratefulness.

NOW, INDEED, We create man out of the essence of clay, and then We cause him to remain as a drop of sperm in [the womb’s) firm keeping, and then We create out of the drop of sperm a germ-cell, and then We create out of the germ-cell an embryonic lump, and then We create within the embryonic lump bones, and then We clothe the bones with flesh - and then We bring [all) this into being as a new creation: hallowed, therefore, is God, the best of artisans! And then, behold! after all this, you are destined to die; and then, behold! you shall be raised from the dead on Resurrection Day. And, indeed, We have created above you seven [celestial] orbits; and never are We unmindful of [any aspect of Our) creation. And We send down water from the skies in accordance with a measure [set by Us], and then We cause it to lodge in the earth: but, behold, We are most certainly able to withdraw this [blessing]! And by means of this [water] We bring forth for you gardens of date-palms and vines, wherein you have fruit abundant and whereof you eat, as well as a tree that issues from [the lands adjoining] Mount Sinai, yielding oil and relish for all to eat. And, behold, in the cattle [too] there is indeed a lesson for you: We give you to drink of that [milk) which is within their bellies; and you derive many [other] uses from them: for, you eat of their flesh, and by them - as by the ships [over the sea) - you are borne [overland]. (23:12-21)

Then begins the story of Noah and other messengers and how they were rejected out of arrogance.

for it is He who has endowed you with hearing, and sight, and minds: [yet] how seldom are you grateful! And He it is who has caused you to multiply on earth; and unto Him you shall be gathered. And He it is who grants life and deals death; and to Him is due the alternation of night and day. Will you not, then, use your reason? (23:78-80)

These are reminders that we are not self-sufficient. However powerful we think we may be, we need God to do what we can do. However wealthy we may be, we need God to attain such wealth. However much we think we know, our senses and intellect are from God. None of those are entirely our own efforts. This is in one of the very first revelations in the Quran:

Nay, verily, man becomes grossly overweening whenever he believes himself to be selfsufficient: for, behold, unto thy Sustainer all must return. (96:6-8)

In my understanding, this is why these verses about nature are placed in the context of people being ungrateful to God and turning away from His messages. I find this a more sensible explanation than scientific “miracles” from the Quran; focusing on scientific “miracles” may distract us from the context that gives the moral (I should write another post to elaborate on why I’m not a fan of finding “science” in the Quran).
